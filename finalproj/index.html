CS184 Final Project Report

Core-Collapse Supernova Simulator
Kailash Ranganathan, Anirudh Kotamraju, Shaoyuan Gao

Abstract

We built a core-collapse supernova simulator that you can actually watch explode. The idea was to model all the main stages of a supernova — the shock launching outward after the star's core collapses, the shock stalling partway out, neutrino heating kicking it back into motion, hot buoyant plumes rising asymmetrically, and finally the star breaking apart. We wrote the whole simulator from scratch in Python, starting with a 2D version and then extending it to 3D. For the visuals we built a rendering pipeline that composites the fluid fields into false-color images for 2D and a pseudo-volumetric projection with depth and lighting cues for 3D. On top of that, as a stretch goal we made a browser-based interactive viewer in Three.js where you can orbit the simulation, scrub through time, and switch between a raymarched volume view and particle views of the ejecta. The final result is something that actually looks like the supernova visualizations you see in research papers.

Technical Approach

The Physics Model

A real supernova simulation requires solving neutrino transport through a collapsing star with a nuclear equation of state — the kind of thing that takes dedicated supercomputers months to run. That wasn't our goal. We wanted something that captures the look of a supernova, so we built what we call a "reduced model": a fluid simulation that includes the most important physical ingredients without any of the hardcore astrophysics machinery.

The state we track at each timestep is a density field, an entropy field, a velocity field, and a bunch of tracer particles. The tracers are basically massless test particles we sprinkle into the fluid to visualize where the material is going — they don't affect the physics, they just tag along with the flow so we can see the ejecta pattern. The whole grid is a cube (or square in 2D) with the proto-neutron star implicitly sitting at the center — we don't simulate the collapse itself, just everything after the shock has launched.

The shock radius has a prescribed trajectory: it expands quickly at first, stalls partway out (around radius 0.27 in our normalized units), hangs there for a bit, and then revives and keeps growing. This mirrors the real "delayed neutrino-driven explosion mechanism" that most current supernova research is focused on. We hardcode this trajectory using a smooth step function, and the actual shock in the simulation relaxes toward it each step rather than snapping. In 3D, the shock surface also gets locally deformed by the entropy structure, so it looks lumpy and asymmetric rather than a perfect sphere.

Heating and cooling work through spatial masks. There's a "gain region" between the shock and an inner gain radius (about 52% of the shock radius) where neutrino heating dominates — we add entropy there each step, weighted by an angular pattern so some directions get more heating than others. Just inside the gain radius there's a cooling zone where entropy is removed. This entropy source/sink is the main driver of the explosion: hot material rises buoyantly, pushing the shock outward asymmetrically.

The velocity update combines a few forces: inward gravity (stronger near the center), a buoyancy force based on how much hotter a cell is than its neighbors in the gain region, an outward push from the shock shell itself, and in 3D a late-time outflow boost along a preferred axis that creates the bipolar jet-like structure seen in some supernova models. We also add a swirl force in 3D perpendicular to the radial direction, which produces the spiral vorticity associated with the standing accretion shock instability (SASI).

For moving the density and entropy fields forward in time we use semi-Lagrangian advection — basically, for each grid cell you trace backward along the velocity for one timestep and sample whatever value was there. The nice thing about this is that it's unconditionally stable, so we can take larger timesteps without things blowing up. Tracer particles are moved by sampling the velocity at their location and integrating with a simple Euler step, with a little drag each step to keep them following the fluid instead of flying off.

Going from 2D to 3D

We designed the simulator to work in both 2D and 3D from the start, which turned out to be a really good call. All the fields are stored as component-stacked arrays that work the same way regardless of dimension, and the advection and interpolation routines just loop over axes. This meant we could debug everything quickly in 2D (which runs much faster) and then just flip a flag to run in 3D.

The 3D version adds a few things the 2D one doesn't have: a preferred symmetry axis that you can tilt and rotate, a random asymmetry axis blended toward it, and a swirl axis for rotation. There's also a "plume seed" — a deterministic spatial noise pattern made from several sine waves at different frequencies that we use to seed variation in the shock surface. This gives us repeatable asymmetric plume shapes without adding random noise every run.

Rendering

For 2D we blend four false-color channels into one composite image: log-density, entropy, heating, and the entropy gradient (which lights up plume boundaries). Each channel uses a different colormap — entropy goes through inferno (red/orange/yellow), heating through turbo (blue/green), and so on. We normalize each channel using percentile-based bounds rather than min/max so that a few extremely hot cells don't wash out everything else. The shock location gets drawn as a white contour on top, and the tracer particles are scattered across as small dots.

For 3D we built a pseudo-volumetric renderer. The idea is to go through the 3D grid from back to front, composite each depth slice onto a canvas with alpha blending, and shift each slice slightly in screen space based on depth to create a sense of perspective. Each voxel gets a color and opacity based on its entropy, heating, and entropy gradient — we specifically avoid making dense material opaque, because early on we did that and the whole image just turned into a featureless glowing sphere. The interesting structure is in the plumes and the shock surface, so those are what we gate the opacity on. After compositing everything, we run a screen-space pass that adds silhouette rim lighting, near/far tint separation (warm foreground, cool background), and a subtle haze. A procedural star field and vignette round out the look.

The camera orbits the domain along one of three paths — a slow orbit, a tight fly-in, and a wide reveal. One thing we had to be careful about: when only rendering every 4th simulation step, the camera phase has to advance by the output frame count, not the raw step count. We got bit by this early on and ended up with visible jumps in the video every time we assembled frames with ffmpeg.

Interactive Web Viewer

As a stretch goal we built a browser-based viewer using Three.js. We export precomputed simulation snapshots to a JSON file containing 3D volume textures, tracer positions, and shock mesh points, then the viewer loads those and renders them in real time. The volume is rendered with a GLSL raymarching shader on a cube mesh; the plume ejecta show up as soft-disc particle sprites; the shock is a smooth mesh. Between saved timesteps, the shader interpolates between adjacent 3D textures so the playback looks smooth even though we only saved 25 frames. You can freely orbit the scene with the mouse, scrub through time with a slider, and switch between a volume view, a particle view, and a tracer ejecta view. There's also a little orientation gizmo in the corner that tracks the camera.

Problems we ran into

Everything looked like a perfect sphere. Our first working simulator produced a beautifully symmetric expanding shock and absolutely nothing else interesting. All the forces were radially symmetric so of course nothing broke that symmetry. We fixed this by adding a low-order angular driving pattern — a mix of a mode-1 sinusoid and a sloshing term — that ramps on after shock stall. In 3D we also added dipole and quadrupole patterns along a tilted preferred axis and the plume seed noise, which finally gave us the asymmetric blobs we were going for.

The 3D render looked like a glowing blob. Our first attempt at the pseudo-volume renderer mapped opacity to density, which made the whole thing look like a smooth orange sphere with nothing inside. We had to completely rethink the transfer function to gate opacity on physical features — entropy gradients at plume interfaces, net heating in the gain region, and a thin band around the shock surface. Once we did that, the internal structure immediately became visible.

Camera jumps in the video. We noticed that every time we assembled an animation, there was a visible snap in the camera motion somewhere around frame 76-80. Turned out the camera orbit phase was using the raw solver step index, but we were only saving every 4th step, so the phase was jumping by 4x too much per rendered frame. Fixing it was a one-liner but took us an embarrassingly long time to diagnose.

Browser volume looked blocky. The first version of the web viewer used a 56³ texture for the raymarched volume, which looked obviously pixelated on any screen. We bumped it up to 96³, applied a light 3D blur to the opacity data before export, and added per-fragment ray-start jitter in the shader to break up the remaining Moiré patterns.

Lessons learned

Writing the solver to be dimension-agnostic from day one saved us a huge amount of pain. We almost wrote separate 2D and 3D codepaths, and that would have been a nightmare to keep in sync. Setting up named presets and a batch runner early also paid off a lot — being able to quickly run "baseline", "showcase", "explosive" and compare them in a few minutes meant we could iterate on visual quality much faster than if we were tweaking parameters by hand each time. On the rendering side, the big lesson was that trying to visualize raw density is almost always wrong for a simulator like this — the interesting physics is in the entropy and heating structure, and you have to let that drive what's visible. And honestly, building the interactive viewer pushed our final quality much higher than it would have been otherwise, because it forced us to fix issues (texture artifacts, choppy playback) that were completely invisible in static PNG frames.

Results

2D Baseline

This is the 2D baseline run on a 192×192 grid. You can see the shock launch at t=0, stall around t=0.72s, then start growing again as the neutrino heating kicks in. By t=2.88s the asymmetric plume structure is fully developed. Colors are entropy (red/yellow), heating (blue/green), with plume boundaries highlighted by the entropy gradient.

Diagnostic plots for the baseline run. The top-left panel shows the shock stall and revival clearly. The asymmetry panels at bottom-right show the low-order modes growing during plume breakout.

3D Showcase

The 3D showcase preset runs on a 72³ grid with a tilted preferred axis, strong dipole asymmetry, swirl, and late-time bipolar outflow. The big panel on the left is the pseudo-volumetric projection; the three small panels are midplane slices (xy, xz, yz) for debugging.

Explosive Preset

We also made an "explosive" preset that cranks up the shock revival speed, ramps down boundary damping, and tilts the preferred axis hard to get a bipolar jet-like explosion. It's more visually dramatic than physically realistic but shows what the simulator can do at the extreme end of the parameter space.

Diagnostics for the explosive preset — notice the much faster shock revival, higher peak entropy, and larger outflow fraction compared to the baseline.

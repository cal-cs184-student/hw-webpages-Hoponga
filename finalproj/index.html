CS184 Final Project Report

Core-Collapse Supernova Simulator
Kailash Ranganathan, Anirudh Kotamraju, Shaoyuan Gao

Abstract

Our project was to make a supernova simulator with offline rendering. We wanted to show the main post-bounce stages. Post-bounce means after the star's core has collapsed and rebounded, launching a shockwave outward. The shock launches, stalls, gets revived by neutrino heating, forms hot plumes, and finally breaks out unevenly. Neutrino heating is basically energy from tiny particles called neutrinos getting deposited back into the star fluid. Plumes are hot blobs of fluid that rise outward, kind of like bubbles in boiling water. We started with the 2D simulator from our milestone and then moved it to 3D. We also made a small browser viewer as a stretch goal, so you can rotate around the saved simulation and scrub through time. The main thing we wanted was a stalled shock that turns into a messy-looking explosion, and that is what the final simulator shows.

Technical Approach

The Physics Model

Like we said in the milestone, supernova dynamics and high energy physics were pretty new to us. We read a bunch of papers and then settled on a post-bounce model. Basically, we do a fluid simulation where the center acts like a proto-neutron star, which is the tiny dense object left in the middle right after collapse. We start after the shock has already launched. The part we care about is after bounce, where the shock, heating, cooling, and plumes matter.

In any dynamical simulation, we have state that gets updated each timestep. In our case, the main state is the density field, entropy field, velocity field, and tracer positions. Density is how much material is there, entropy is a rough way to track how hot/energetic the material is, and velocity is how fast it is moving and in what direction. The tracers are basically marker particles that exist to show the flow of the star fluid. They ride along with the velocity field so we can see where material is going.

The way we simulate the shock is still similar to the milestone version. The shock is the outward-moving pressure wave that starts the explosion. We hardcode the rough shock radius over time, where the radius is just how far the shock is from the center. It grows outward, stalls for a bit, and then keeps going after the revival. This comes from the delayed neutrino-driven explosion mechanism that a lot of supernova papers talk about. The shock first loses steam, then neutrino heating helps it start moving outward again. In 3D, we also let the entropy field deform the shock surface locally, which gives the shock a much more uneven shape.

The shock shell is the thin area around the shock surface. It divides the grid into different regions. Outside the shock, the fluid evolves with the normal fluid update. Near the shock and gain region, we add heating to represent neutrino energy being deposited back into the star fluid. The gain region is the area where the fluid gains energy from neutrino heating. Closer to the center, we remove some entropy to represent cooling. The heating also varies by angle, which helps break the symmetry and makes the plumes more interesting.

The velocity update has a few main forces. We have inward gravity from the center, buoyancy from hot high-entropy fluid, a shock push around the shock shell, and in 3D a swirl/outflow term that bends the flow away from being purely radial. Buoyancy is the same idea as hot air rising, and high-entropy fluid is our hot energetic fluid. Radial means straight outward from the center. The buoyancy part is important because it is what makes hot plumes rise and push the shock into those uneven shapes.

For moving density and entropy forward, we use semi-Lagrangian advection. The basic idea is that for each grid cell, we look backward along the velocity field and sample the old value from there. Advection means carrying stuff along with the flow. Semi-Lagrangian is the backwards lookup trick that makes the update stable. The tracer particles are moved by sampling the velocity where they are and taking a simple Euler step.

Going from 2D to 3D

We originally settled for a 2D fluid simulation, imagining the star as a cylinder and the shockwave as a circle expanding outwards. That was much easier to debug, so we kept using 2D as our quick test case. Once that worked, we moved the same ideas to 3D by making the arrays and advection routines work for either dimension.

The 3D version adds a few extra controls: a preferred direction for the explosion, a swirl direction, and a deterministic plume seed. The plume seed is a fixed pattern made from sine waves. Since it is deterministic, the same settings make the same seed every time. It gives the shock small bumps that grow into repeatable asymmetric plumes.

Rendering

For 2D rendering, we combine a few fields into one image. Entropy gives the red/yellow hot-looking colors, heating adds blue/green, and entropy gradients make the plume boundaries pop. A gradient shows how quickly a value changes from one place to the next, so entropy gradients show the edges between hot plumes and the surrounding material. We also draw the shock as a white outline and scatter the tracer particles on top. This is basically the same style as the milestone frames, with cleaner colors and more consistent scaling.

For 3D rendering, we stack slices of the 3D grid from back to front and blend them onto the image. At first we made dense regions opaque, but that looked terrible because it became one smooth glowing ball. What actually looks good is showing the plume edges, the heated region, and the shock shell. After that, we add some rim lighting around the edge, plus color tinting and a star field so the final frames are easier to read.

We also added camera paths for videos. One annoying bug was that the camera used the raw solver step even though we saved every 4th frame. So the camera would randomly jump in the video. The fix was to move the camera based on the output frame number instead.

Interactive Web Viewer

As a stretch goal, we made a Three.js viewer. We export saved snapshots to JSON and load them in the browser. You can rotate around the result, move through time with a slider, and switch between the volume-looking view, plume particles, and tracer particles. It is a nicer way to inspect the run than flipping through PNGs.

Problems we ran into

Everything looked like a perfect sphere. Our first working simulator made a clean expanding circle/sphere. That was neat for about five seconds, but the supernova needed more structure. All of our forces were radial, so of course nothing interesting happened. We fixed this by adding angular heating patterns and plume seeds so some parts of the shock get pushed more than others.

The 3D render looked like a glowing blob. Our first 3D render made opacity depend on density. Opacity means how see-through something is. This was a bad idea. The whole image turned into a smooth orange ball, and all the actual plume detail disappeared. We changed opacity to depend more on plume boundaries, heating, and the shock band, which made the structure show up.

Camera jumps in the video. We noticed that every time we made a video, the camera snapped around frame 76 to 80. It took us a while to figure out, but the issue was just that the camera was using the wrong frame counter. It was using solver steps instead of saved output frames.

Browser volume looked blocky. The first version of the web viewer used a 56³ texture, and it looked pretty blocky. We raised it to 96³, blurred the opacity data a little before export, and added a small amount of shader jitter. That made it look a lot closer to the offline frames.

Lessons learned

The biggest thing we learned is that writing the simulator in a general enough way saved us a lot of time. We could debug fast in 2D and then run the same idea in 3D. Presets also helped a ton because otherwise we would have been manually changing numbers forever. On the rendering side, we learned that raw density by itself is a bad visual cue. Entropy, heating, and plume boundaries are much better for seeing what is happening.

Results

2D Baseline

This is our 2D baseline run on a 192x192 grid. You can see the shock launch, stall around t=0.72s, and then start expanding again once the heating kicks in. By the last frame, the plume structure is pretty clear. Red/yellow is entropy, blue/green is heating, and the lighter edges are plume boundaries.

The diagnostics show the same thing in plot form. The shock radius stalls and then revives, and the asymmetry plots grow as the plumes break out.

3D Showcase

This is the main 3D run we wanted to show. It runs on a 72³ grid and uses a tilted axis, stronger asymmetry, swirl, and late outflow. The tilted axis is the direction the explosion prefers to stretch along. The big panel is the 3D-looking render. The smaller panels are slices through the grid, which helped us debug what was happening inside.

Explosive Preset

We also made an "explosive" preset. This one revives the shock faster, lowers the boundary damping, and tilts the preferred axis more strongly. Boundary damping is how much we slow things down near the edge of the simulation box so they do not behave weirdly at the border. This preset is the more dramatic version, and it was useful to see how much we could push the simulator.

The explosive diagnostics show what we expected: faster shock revival, higher peak entropy, and more outflow than the baseline.

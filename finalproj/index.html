CS184 Final Project Report

Core-Collapse Supernova Simulator
Kailash Ranganathan, Anirudh Kotamraju, Shaoyuan Gao

Abstract

We built a real-time-parameterizable, offline-rendered simulator of core-collapse supernova (CCSN) dynamics. Starting from the known post-bounce physics — a stalled shock, a neutrino-driven gain region, buoyancy-driven convective plumes, and asymmetric shock revival — we implemented a reduced compressible fluid model on a Cartesian grid in both 2D and 3D. The simulator evolves density, entropy, and velocity fields forward with semi-Lagrangian advection, explicit heating and cooling source terms, and a prescribed shock-radius trajectory. Thousands of massless tracer particles track the ejecta flow. For visualization we built a layered rendering pipeline: a false-color composite of scalar fields for 2D runs, and a pseudo-volumetric depth-composited projection with perspective, rim lighting, and cinematic depth cues for 3D. On top of that we built a browser-based interactive viewer using Three.js that lets users scrub through simulation time, rotate the camera, and toggle between raymarched volume, plume particle, and shock-surface views. Together the result is a visually convincing, parameterizable animation of all the main stages of a supernova explosion.

Technical Approach

Physics Model

A realistic CCSN simulation requires solving the Boltzmann neutrino transport equation coupled to multi-fluid MHD with a nuclear equation of state — a problem that consumes hundreds of millions of CPU-hours on dedicated supercomputers. Our goal was a qualitatively correct visual simulator, so we adopted a reduced model that captures the essential dynamics without any of that machinery.

The physical state at each timestep consists of four fields defined on a uniform Cartesian grid: a density field \(\rho\), an entropy field \(s\), a vector velocity field \(\mathbf{v}\), and the positions and velocities of \(N\) massless tracer particles. The grid spans a normalized domain of radius 1 (roughly 500 km in physical units). We do not simulate the core collapse itself — the simulation begins post-bounce, with the proto-neutron star treated implicitly as a fixed gravitational source at the center and a prescribed neutrino-heating zone.

The shock trajectory is prescribed analytically via a smoothstep function: the shock radius \(R_\text{shock}(t)\) first expands rapidly, stalls near \(R \approx 0.27\) for a fixed interval (representing the energy-loss phase), then revives and expands to a final radius of \(\approx 0.8\). At each timestep the actual shock radius relaxes toward the target with a first-order lag, and a locally deformed shock surface \(R_\text{shock}(\hat{\mathbf{n}})\) is built by modulating the mean radius with a low-order angular pattern seeded by the entropy field.

Heating and cooling are defined by spatial masks. The gain region — the annular shell between the gain radius (≈ 52% of the shock radius) and the shock — receives a heating term proportional to \(\exp(-2r)\) and weighted by a low-order angular driving pattern, mimicking the direction-dependent neutrino deposition expected from 3D simulations. A narrow cooling mask just inside the gain radius removes entropy, representing the net-cooling zone seen below the gain radius in real neutrino-transport models. The net source term \[ \dot{s} = Q_\text{heat} - Q_\text{cool} + \kappa \nabla^2 s \] is added to the entropy field each step; a small Laplacian diffusion term \(\kappa \nabla^2 s\) stabilizes the solver against grid-scale noise.

The velocity update combines four radial forces — gravity \(-g/(r^2 + \epsilon)\), a buoyancy term proportional to local entropy contrast relative to the gain-region mean, an outward shock-push Gaussian peaked at the shock surface, and (in 3D) a late-time outflow bias along a preferred symmetry axis — plus a pressure-gradient acceleration \(-\nabla P / \rho\) where the pressure equation of state is \(P = \rho (0.55 + 1.6\,s)\). In 3D, a swirl force is added perpendicular to both the radial direction and a preferred rotation axis, producing the spiral vorticity seen in SASI-dominated simulations. The velocity is advanced with a simple explicit step and a mild global damping factor of 0.992 to prevent unbounded growth.

Advection of density and entropy is done with semi-Lagrangian back-tracing: for each grid cell, we trace a streamline backward by one timestep and sample the upwind field value using N-dimensional multilinear interpolation over the \(2^N\) corner stencil. This scheme is unconditionally stable regardless of the Courant number, which allowed us to use relatively large timesteps (\(\Delta t = 0.012\)) without numerical blow-up. Tracer particles are advected by sampling the velocity field at their positions with the same interpolant and integrating with an Euler step; a momentum relaxation (drag) damps their velocity toward the local grid velocity each step so they track the fluid rather than free-streaming.

2D and 3D Implementations

We designed the solver to be dimension-agnostic from the start. All coordinate grids, radial unit vectors, and velocity arrays are stored as \((D \times N^D)\)-shaped component-stacked arrays. The semi-Lagrangian advection routine, the multilinear sampler, and the Laplacian all work by iterating over axes, so the same code runs in 2D and 3D without branching. This let us validate correctness in fast 2D runs before committing to slow 3D ones.

In 3D, the model gains a preferred symmetry axis (configurable tilt and azimuth), a random asymmetry axis blended toward it, a swirl axis for rotation, and a structured plume seed — a deterministic spatial noise field built from sines at several spatial frequencies to seed angular variation in the shock surface without introducing random noise per run.

Rendering

For 2D output we build a composite image by blending four false-color channels — log-density mapped through the cividis colormap, entropy through inferno, heating through turbo, and the entropy gradient magnitude through magma — with weights 0.24 / 0.34 / 0.24 / 0.12, plus a thin shock-rim highlight. All channels are normalized using robust percentile bounds (not min/max) so that a few hot cells do not wash out the plume structure. A contour at the shock level set overlays the composite, and tracers are drawn as small scatter points.

For 3D output we implemented a pseudo-volumetric projection. The 3D grid is traversed from back to front; each depth slice is mapped to a per-voxel emission color and opacity using a transfer function that gates on physical features: entropy, net heating, entropy-gradient magnitude (which highlights plume interfaces), and a thin shock-surface band. Feature gating was critical — early attempts assigned opacity to raw density, which collapsed the whole image to a featureless bright blob. Each depth slice is scaled by a mild perspective factor, shifted obliquely in screen space according to a camera-pose phase, and composited front-to-back with the standard alpha-blending formula \(C = C_\text{prev} + (1 - \alpha_\text{prev}) \alpha_k C_k\). After compositing, a screen-space post-pass adds silhouette rim lighting, near/far tint separation, and a subtle depth-haze term. A simulated deep-space background with procedural stars completes the image. A cinematic vignette and gamma curve are applied last.

Camera paths orbit the domain along one of three named trajectories (slow_orbit, tight_fly_in, wide_reveal); the camera phase advances by output cadence — not by raw solver step — so sparse renders do not exhibit phase jumps.

Interactive Web Viewer

We exported precomputed simulation snapshots as compact JSON containing 3D entropy/heating/opacity arrays, tracer positions, and shock-surface points. A static Three.js viewer at web/interactive_viewer/ loads this data and renders it in the browser with a GLSL raymarching shader on a cube volume, soft-disc particle sprites for plume ejecta, and a smooth shock mesh. Shader-side interpolation between adjacent saved time slices produces smooth in-between frames at 300 ms/step playback. OrbitControls let the user freely rotate the scene; a compact XYZ gizmo tracks camera orientation. The viewer exposes a time scrubber, play/pause, and a view selector for volume, plume particles, and tracer ejecta.

Problems and How We Tackled Them

Spherical symmetry. The initial solver produced perfectly spherical shocks because all forces were radially symmetric. We broke this by adding a low-order angular driving pattern — a superposition of a mode-1 sinusoid and a sloshing term — ramped on smoothly after shock stall. In 3D we additionally introduced dipole and quadrupole projections onto a tilted preferred axis and a structured plume seed, which produces consistent asymmetric plume geometry across runs.

Volume rendering opacity. Assigning opacity to log-density produced a uniformly bright sphere with no internal structure. We switched to feature-gated opacity (entropy, heating, entropy gradient, thin shock rim) which immediately revealed plume structure. Getting the opacity scale right required iterative tuning per transfer-function term.

Camera phase jumps. When rendering only every 4th solver step, the camera orbit phase was advancing by the raw step index rather than the output frame index, causing a visible snap every time the scene was assembled into a video. We fixed this by passing the output-cadence frame index to the camera pose resolver rather than the solver step.

Browser volume blockiness. The initial web export used a 56³ texture, which looked obviously voxelated in the browser. We increased the export resolution to 96³, applied a separable 3D blur to the opacity proxy before export, and added per-fragment ray-start jitter in the GLSL shader to break up Moiré patterns.

Lessons Learned

Dimension-agnostic design from the start was essential — retrofitting 3D support into a 2D-only codebase would have been far more painful. Similarly, investing in named presets and a batch runner early let us iterate on visual quality quickly instead of retyping parameters. On the rendering side, we learned that feature-gated opacity (driven by physical signals like entropy and heating) is the right abstraction: attempts to visualize raw density or to dial in a single global opacity scale were always wrong in different regions of the domain simultaneously. Finally, building the interactive web viewer uncovered a class of issues (texture blockiness, interpolation artifacts) that were invisible in static PNG frames — shipping an interactive artifact pushed us toward a substantially higher final quality.
